# Responsive neuromodulation in temporal lobe epilepsy: Preclinical investigation of the safety and efficacy of the WiChR opsin as an optogenetic tool

**Master's Thesis — MSc in Biomedical Sciences**

*   **Authors:** Hendrik Willems & Brin Bogeart
*   **Supervisors:** Prof. Dr. Robrecht Raedt, Sofie Lasure, and Lien De Schaepmeester
*   **Institution:** 4Brain Lab (Department of Head and Skin), Ghent University (UGent)

---

## Abstract

### Background
Temporal Lobe Epilepsy (TLE) is the most common and drug-resistant form of epilepsy, emphasizing the need for alternative treatments. Responsive neuromodulation (RNM) using optogenetics is a promising approach that employs genetically encoded, light-sensitive opsins to modulate neural activity in real-time with high temporal and spatial precision. WiChR, a novel inhibitory opsin, holds potential for seizure suppression, but its safety and efficacy remain to be validated.

### Methods
A dose-titration study was conducted using three dilutions (1/5, 1/10, 1/100) of the AAV vector encoding WiChR to assess neurotoxicity in the mouse hippocampus. Histological evaluation focused on markers of hippocampal degeneration and microglial activation. The safest dilution (1/100) was subsequently used in the intrahippocampal kainic acid (IHKA) mouse model for TLE. This dilution was then used in an RNM experiment to assess seizure suppression.

### Results
A dose-dependent increase in hippocampal degeneration and microgliosis was observed at higher viral titers (1/5, 1/10), while the 1/100 dilution showed no signs of toxicity. The addition of WiChR to the IHKA mouse model did not induce neurotoxicity beyond that caused by KA alone. Following improved WiChR expression using the Nanoliter injector instead of a Hamilton syringe, optogenetic activation significantly reduced seizure duration.

### Conclusion
This study identified a safe and effective AAV-WiChR dose that enables seizure suppression in a mouse model of TLE, thereby validating the potential of WiChR-based neuromodulation. These findings support further investigation into WiChR for clinical application and highlight the importance of dosing precision and delivery technique in optogenetic therapy.
